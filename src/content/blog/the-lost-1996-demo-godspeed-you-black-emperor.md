A recently unearthed cassette demo from 1996 has forced a quiet revision of Godspeed You! Black Emperor's early chronology. The tape, dated two years before the official release of *F♯ A♯ ∞*, bears a title that looks more like a typographic accident than a record name: **¥∞♯δA** — a string of five symbols whose only common ground is the difficulty of typing them.

According to sources close to the band's early circle, the original title was assembled during a late-night session at the Hotel2Tango studio in Montreal. "We wanted something that felt illegible, untrackable," a former associate recalled. "Something that couldn't be searched, couldn't be indexed. It was pure spite against the idea of discoverability."

The decision to rebrand the work as *F♯ A♯ ∞* for its 1997 Constellation Records pressing was, ironically, driven by the opposite impulse. "We realised nobody could find the damn thing," the same source claims. "Discogs couldn't list it. Record stores didn't know where to file it. The label asked us to change it to something a search engine could parse."

## Two Lost Tracks

The 1996 demo contains two pieces that did not survive to the final release. The first, titled **"Missile Fall on Missile Fall"**, is a sixteen-minute dirge built around a descending guitar motif and a sampled recitation about Cold War escalation. While the track was omitted from *F♯ A♯ ∞*, the band revisited the material during the *Yanqui U.X.O.* sessions, reworking it into what would eventually be titled **"Rockets Fall on Rocket Falls"**. The structure is recognisably the same — the same intervals, the same arc — but the production is cleaner, the samples replaced, and the title's grammar deliberately broken.

The second orphan track is listed simply as **"Redeemer"** — a skeletal, feedback-drenched piece running just over nine minutes. This would later be expanded and retitled **"Motherfucker=Redeemer"**, released as the closing track of *Yanqui U.X.O.* in 2002. The demo version lacks the field recordings and orchestral swells of the final cut, but the melodic core is unmistakable.

## "A Landscape After Impact"

In a 2001 interview with the now-defunct Montreal zine *Static Discharge* — a copy of which was shared alongside the demo — guitarist Efrim Menuck reportedly described the conceptual thread linking the band's first two albums:

> "We were thinking about what happens to a place after it's been hit. Not just physically — the craters, the glass, the burned trees — but psychically. What does a forest sound like when it knows it's going to be bombed? What does a river remember? *F♯ A♯ ∞* [he uses the original ¥∞♯δA title in the transcript] is the moment before. *Yanqui U.X.O.* is the moment after. Everything between is just the fallout."

Whether or not the demo ever receives an official release, the tape confirms that the core concerns of Godspeed You! Black Emperor — ruined landscapes, militarised borders, the quiet before destruction — were in place from the very first recording. The only thing that changed was the name.

A digitised transfer of the ¥∞♯δA cassette is circulating among collectors. No official statement from Constellation Records or the band has been issued.
